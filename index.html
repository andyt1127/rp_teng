Effect of different-sized dimples on the drag coefficient of bicycle helmets

Created by: Andy Teng

Abstract

This study investigated whether different-sized dimples on the same helmet would have significant differences in their drag coefficient. The study was conducted by measuring the drag force of two helmets with different-sized dimples and one control with no dimples using a force sensor. All three helmets have the same dimensions and mass. The drag force data was transformed into drag coefficients through the drag equation. The helmet with small dimples showed a significant decrease in drag coefficient while the helmet with large dimples showed a significant increase in drag coefficient. The results of the experiment suggest that increased dimple sizes would increase the drag coefficient.

Background

drag is classified as a mechanical force acting opposite to the relative motion of any given object. In the cycling industry, reducing drag is critical to enhancing a cyclistâ€™s performance and efficiency. With the same amount of force applied, a cyclist whose equipment experiences lower drag would be able to achieve higher speeds due to having less force to overcome. One way designers reduce drag is by adding dimples. Dimples have been scientifically proven to reduce drag. Dimples create a non-smooth surface, which results in a turbulent layer. By adding turbulence through dimples, the layer can better resist the separation, which produces less drag.

Rationale

Previously conducted research on the topic of applying dimples on objects has tested whether dimples could reduce the air resistance of objects, however, little research has been done on objects that are not fully enclosed, such as bicycle helmets. By using an object such as a bicycle helmet, a better understanding could be gained of whether dimples could reduce drag in an unclosed object compared to a closed object
